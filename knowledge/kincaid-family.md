Sarah Whitmore Kincaid Massey Johnson
Role: Jake Kincaid's mother
Family:
Mother of Jake Kincaid.
First wife of Thomas Kincaid.
Second wife of Victor Massey.
Later, the third wife of Gilbert Johnson
Jake never knew that she and Victor eventually divorced, and that she later remarried to a good man this time. She never actually received the photos he’d sent; Victor did.
Core Spine:
Sarah loved her son but never found the courage to protect him from the men she married. Fear, dependence, and exhaustion slowly replaced the strength she once possessed.
Background:
Sarah married Thomas Kincaid as a young woman and gave birth to their only child, Jake. When Jake was five years old, Thomas abandoned the family, leaving Sarah to raise him alone. Seeking stability, she later married Victor Massey. Instead of finding security, she brought Jake into a home ruled by violence. Victor's abuse became a normal part of daily life while Sarah remained silent, convincing herself there was little she could do. After Victor, she eventually married Gilbert Johnson.
Jake never forgot that his mother failed to stand between him and the men who hurt him. Although he understood how difficult her circumstances had been, the wound never completely healed.
Core Beliefs:
A child needs a family, even if that family is imperfect.
Survival sometimes requires silence.
Peace is often purchased by avoiding confrontation.
Greatest Strength:
Sarah possessed quiet endurance and an ability to survive hardship.
Fatal Flaw:
She chose endurance over protection when Jake needed her most.
Psychological Importance:
Sarah taught Jake that love without protection is not enough. Her silence shaped the father Jake later became, determining that his own children would never have to fear being in the same room with him or wonder whether he would stand beside them.
Relationship With Jake:
Jake loved his mother once, but her refusal to protect him ended that relationship long before he left home. After walking away at thirteen, he never saw her again. Years later, he mailed photographs of himself, Krys, and Kai, and the life he had built, but included no letter. He wanted her to know he had survived, that he had built the family and home she never gave him, and that he had done alright without her. He expected nothing in return.
Locked Thematic Core:
Love weakened by fear.
Thomas Kincaid
Role: Jake Kincaid's biological father
Family:
First husband of Sarah Whitmore.
Father of Jake Kincaid.
Core Spine:
Thomas chose himself over his family, leaving his wife and young son to face life without him.
Background:
Thomas married Sarah Whitmore and fathered Jake. When Jake was five years old, Thomas abandoned his wife and child, disappearing from their lives. His departure forced Sarah to seek another husband and ultimately placed Jake in the household where Victor Massey became his abuser.
Thomas never returned to raise his son.
Core Beliefs:
Personal freedom outweighs responsibility.
A difficult life is easier to leave than endure.
Greatest Strength:
Thomas possessed the confidence to walk away from difficult circumstances.
Fatal Flaw:
He abandoned the people who depended on him.
Psychological Importance:
Thomas became the first example of abandonment in Jake's life. Long before Victor taught Jake what cruelty looked like, Thomas taught him what desertion looked like. Jake spent the rest of his life proving that a man stays with his family no matter how difficult life becomes.
Relationship With Jake:
Jake has no meaningful memories of his father beyond the fact that he left. Every promise Jake keeps to Krys, Kai, and Wes stands in quiet opposition to Thomas's choice.
Locked Thematic Core:
Abandonment creates generations determined never to repeat it.
Jake Kincaid  
Given Name: Jacob Weston Kincaid
Born: November 3, 1866
Role: Founder of Five Oaks • Enforcer • Anchor
Family:
Husband of Krys Callahan Kincaid.
Father of Kai Weston Kincaid and Wes Kincaid.
Core Spine:
Jake protects. Everything else is secondary. His entire life is built around creating a home where the people he loves never have to live in fear again. Silence became his armor after years of abuse, and violence became a tool to end danger—not prolong it. He believes loyalty is absolute once earned, cruelty is weakness, and suffering is easier to carry alone than to place on someone else's shoulders.
Childhood Foundation:
Jake grew up with an absent father, an emotionally distant mother, an abusive stepfather, and abusive stepbrothers. His mother never intervened. At thirteen, he chose hunger over another day in that house and left home to survive on his own.
Core Formation:
Pain lasts longer when you react.
Silence is armor.
End conflict quickly before it grows.
Core Beliefs:
Loyalty is absolute once earned.
Violence must be clean and necessary.
Cruelty is weakness.
Family always comes first.
A man protects; he never intimidates.
Greatest Fear:
Becoming the kind of man who filled his childhood home with fear, especially in the eyes of his own children.
Greatest Strength:
Unshakable loyalty, emotional steadiness, and quiet leadership.
Fatal Flaw:
Carries every burden himself and rarely allows others to share his pain.
Psychological Markers:
Speaks only when necessary.
Rarely wastes movement.
Ends fights rather than escalating them.
Never enjoys violence.
Constantly measures himself against the men who abused him.
Locked Thematic Core:
Finality born from abuse and silence.
Victor Massey
Role: Jake Kincaid’s abusive stepfather who raised Jake for five years, from age eight to age thirteen, when he left home.
Family:
Stepfather of Jake Kincaid.
Father of Dwayne, Curtis, and Albert Massey.
Core Spine:
Victor ruled his household through fear, punishment, and control. He expected obedience and allowed his sons to help make Jake the family target.
Background:
Victor married Jake’s mother and became the dominant authority in Jake’s childhood home. He abused Jake, while Jake’s mother failed to intervene. Curtis frequently provoked trouble or blamed Jake, knowing Victor would punish him. Life under Victor taught Jake to remain quiet, read danger early, and end conflict before it could grow.
Jake left the household at thirteen because poverty and hunger felt safer than remaining under Victor’s control.
Core Beliefs:
Authority gives a man the right to punish.
Fear creates obedience.
A child who resists must be broken down.
Power matters more than fairness.
Greatest Strength:
He knew how to dominate a household and make everyone inside it aware of his temper.
Fatal Flaw:
Victor confused cruelty with authority and obedience with respect.
Psychological Importance:
Victor became the example Jake spent his entire adult life refusing to follow. Jake’s fear of frightening Krys or his sons comes directly from growing up under him.
Relationship With Jake:
Victor was Jake’s primary childhood abuser. Jake does not regard him as family and measures his own conduct against Victor’s throughout his life.
Locked Thematic Core:
Authority corrupted by cruelty.
Curtis Massey
Role: Jake Kincaid’s middle stepbrother
Family:
Son of Victor.
Middle brother among Victor’s sons.
Stepbrother of Jake Kincaid.
Married; his wife’s family lives near enough to Five Oaks for him to visit them.
Core Spine:
Curtis survived Victor’s household by directing trouble toward Jake. As a boy, he repeatedly caused problems or blamed Jake because he knew Victor would punish him.
Background:
Curtis grew up in the same violent household as Jake, but he held a safer place because he was Victor’s son. Rather than protect Jake, he used him as a shield whenever possible.
Years later, Curtis visited Five Oaks after hearing that Jake was one of its owners. He approached Jake while the family was working on Marie and Rhys’s cabin. Jake immediately rejected him, said Curtis was no brother of his, and ordered him away.
Curtis’s appearance brought Jake’s childhood memories back so powerfully that Jake withdrew from his family, drank heavily, snapped at Kai, and feared he had behaved like Victor.
Core Beliefs:
Protect yourself first.
Someone else can carry the punishment.
Blood creates a claim even when affection never existed.
Greatest Strength:
Curtis learned how to read Victor and avoid becoming the target of his anger.
Fatal Flaw:
He protected himself by sacrificing Jake.
Psychological Markers:
Uses familiarity as though the past can be approached casually.
Does not appear to understand how deeply his actions marked Jake.
Believes the stepbrother connection still gives him some place in Jake’s life.
His adult motives remain unclear; the book does not establish whether he came from curiosity, regret, envy, or some mixture of them.
Relationship With Jake:
Curtis regards Jake as a stepbrother from his childhood. Jake regards Curtis as part of the household he escaped and refuses to call him a brother.
Locked Thematic Core:
Self-preservation purchased with another child’s suffering.
Dwayne Massey
Role: Jake Kincaid’s stepbrother
Family:
Oldest son of Victor.
Brother of Curtis and Albert.
Stepbrother of Jake Kincaid.
Core Spine:
Dwayne grew up benefiting from a household structure that placed Jake beneath Victor’s sons. Whether he actively caused every punishment or merely accepted Jake’s suffering, he remained part of the family force Jake had to escape.
Background:
Dwayne was one of Victor’s three sons and participated in the abusive family environment that shaped Jake’s childhood. Victor favored his biological sons, leaving Jake isolated and vulnerable.
Core Beliefs:
Victor’s authority should not be challenged.
Jake occupied the lowest place in the household.
Protecting a stepbrother was less important than remaining in Victor’s favor.
Greatest Strength:
Dwayne understood how to survive beneath Victor’s rule.
Fatal Flaw:
He accepted cruelty when it benefited him.
Psychological Importance:
Dwayne represents the wider household that watched Jake suffer and failed to protect him.
Relationship With Jake:
Jake does not regard Dwayne as a brother. Dwayne belongs to the family Jake rejected when he left home at thirteen.
Locked Thematic Core:
Complicity born from comfort and fear.
Albert Massey
Role: Jake Kincaid’s stepbrother
Family:
Youngest son of Victor.
Brother of Curtis and Dwayne.
Stepbrother of Jake Kincaid.
Core Spine:
Albert was raised inside the same hierarchy that protected Victor’s sons while making Jake expendable.
Background:
Albert grew up alongside Curtis, Dwayne, and Jake in Victor’s household. Like his brothers, he benefited from Victor treating Jake as an outsider and target.
Core Beliefs:
Blood gives greater standing within the household.
Victor’s temper is best survived by staying away from its path.
Jake’s punishment is not his responsibility.
Greatest Strength:
Albert learned how to function within Victor’s rules.
Fatal Flaw:
He failed to recognize Jake’s suffering as something he had a duty to oppose.
Psychological Importance:
Albert helps complete the family system Jake escaped. Victor caused the violence, but the three stepbrothers helped make isolation part of Jake’s daily life.
Relationship With Jake:
Their shared childhood does not create brotherhood in Jake’s eyes. Albert remains connected to the life Jake chose never to return to.
Locked Thematic Core:
Brotherhood denied by cruelty and indifference.
