Elias Rawlins
Role: Luke Rawlins' father
Family:
Husband of Lydia Mercer Rawlins.
Father of Luke Rawlins.
Father of Astrid Rawlins.
Father of Iris Rawlins.
Core Spine:
Elias ruled his family through alcohol, fear, and violence. Every member of his household learned to anticipate his temper because no one knew what would provoke it next.
Background:
Elias Rawlins drank heavily and regularly beat his wife. As Luke grew older, he increasingly stepped between his father and the rest of the family, deliberately drawing the beatings onto himself whenever he could. Astrid and Iris survived much of their childhood because Luke learned to redirect Elias's attention before it reached them.
The older Luke became, the more often he became the preferred target. At fourteen, after a beating that convinced him remaining in the house would eventually get someone killed, Luke walked away and never returned. Elias lost the son who had spent years shielding the rest of the family from him.
Core Beliefs:
Fear creates obedience.
Strength is proven through control.
A father has the right to rule his household however he sees fit.
Compassion is weakness.
Greatest Strength:
He knew how to intimidate everyone around him into doing as he wished.
Fatal Flaw:
He confused fear with respect and cruelty with authority.
Psychological Importance:
Elias became the man Luke spent the rest of his life refusing to become. Every child Luke protects, every joke he tells to ease tension, and every attempt he makes to calm a room stand in direct opposition to his father's example.
Relationship With Luke:
Luke spent his childhood protecting his mother and sisters from Elias whenever he could. After leaving home at fourteen, he never regarded him as a father again.
Locked Thematic Core:
Authority corrupted by violence.
Lydia Mercer Rawlins
Role: Luke Rawlins' mother
Family:
Wife of Elias Rawlins.
Mother of Luke Rawlins.
Mother of Astrid Rawlins.
Mother of Iris Rawlins.
Daughter of Lucas Mercer, for whom Luke was named.
Core Spine:
Lydia loved her children deeply but spent so many years living under fear that survival became all she knew. She could not protect them because she had long since lost the ability to protect herself.
Background:
Lydia Mercer married Elias Rawlins believing she was building a family. Instead, she found herself trapped in a violent marriage that steadily stripped away her confidence and independence. She named her firstborn son Lucas after her own father, hoping he would grow into the kind, dependable man her father had been.
As Elias's drinking and violence worsened, Luke began placing himself between his father and the rest of the family. Lydia watched her son endure beatings meant for her and for his younger sisters, Astrid and Iris, knowing she lacked the strength to stop them. When Luke finally left home at fourteen, she understood why he had gone, even though watching him leave broke her heart.
Core Beliefs:
Children deserve better than the lives they inherit.
Kindness is never wasted.
Hope can survive even in terrible places.
Greatest Strength:
Her love for her children never disappeared, even after years of abuse.
Fatal Flaw:
Years of violence left her unable to protect the children she loved most.
Psychological Importance:
Lydia taught Luke compassion, while her helplessness taught him that someone must always stand between violence and those unable to defend themselves. Together, those lessons became the foundation of the man he grew into.
Relationship With Luke:
Luke never blamed his mother for the abuse they suffered. He understood she was another victim of Elias's violence, but watching her endure it convinced him that if anyone was going to stand between his father and the rest of the family, it had to be him.
Locked Thematic Core:
Love imprisoned by fear.
Luke Rawlins 
Given Name: Lucas Paul Rawlins
Born: August 11, 1868
Role: Founder of Five Oaks • Charmer • Distraction
Family:
Husband of Emma Richards Rawlins.
Father of Samantha "Sami" Rawlins Kincaid.
Core Spine:
Luke protects people by carrying the attention himself. Humor, charm, and easy conversation became shields long before they became personality. If people are watching him, they are not hurting someone else.
Childhood Foundation:
Luke grew up with a violent, alcoholic father who regularly abused his mother. He also had two younger sisters, Astrid and Iris, whom he constantly tried to protect by redirecting his father's attention toward himself. He finally left home at fourteen after a beating that convinced him staying would eventually get someone killed.
Core Formation:
Charm redirects violence.
Staying creates targets.
Leave before attachment becomes leverage.
If it can’t be prevented, take the beating before someone else-someone he cares about-has to.
Core Beliefs:
Protect children at all costs.
Ease tension whenever possible.
Laughter keeps despair from taking over.
Better to carry the burden yourself.
Greatest Fear:
Someone he loves becomes a victim because of him.
Greatest Strength:
Empathy, adaptability, emotional intelligence, and optimism.
Fatal Flaw:
Uses humor to avoid confronting painful emotions and sometimes manipulates situations, believing he's helping.
Psychological Markers:
Hyperaware of changing moods.
Deflects tension naturally.
Uses humor to calm others.
Reads rooms almost instantly.
Cannot tolerate abuse toward children.
Widow Incident:
Luke genuinely believed arranging companionship for Matt would help him heal. Only later did he realize he had unintentionally taken away Matt's sense of control, and the realization filled him with genuine guilt.
Locked Thematic Core:
Charm born from violence and protection.
Astrid Rawlins
Role: Luke Rawlins’s younger sister
Family:
Younger sister of Luke Rawlins.
Older sister of Iris Rawlins.
Wife of Earl.
Mother of Alison.
Core Spine:
Astrid survived her childhood by hardening herself, but the grief she carried eventually turned into anger toward Luke. She remembers him less as the fourteen-year-old boy who escaped and more as the older brother who left her behind.
Background:
Astrid was twelve when Luke left home at fourteen. Iris was ten. All three children lived under a violent alcoholic father who abused their mother and children. Luke frequently redirected their father’s attention toward himself to protect the girls.
After Luke left, Astrid remained trapped in the household with Iris. She later married Earl, another harsh and abusive man, and had a daughter, Alison.
When Tom told her Luke was married and living near Red Willow Crossing, Astrid eventually traveled to Five Oaks with Alison. She arrived thinner than Luke remembered, with a fading bruise on her face. During her stay, she repeatedly blamed Luke for leaving and told him Iris had cried for him for months. Krys finally confronted her, reminding her that Luke had only been fourteen and could not have saved them from their father.
Core Beliefs:
People who leave do not understand what happens to those left behind.
Depending on someone gives them the power to abandon you.
Pain must belong to somebody.
Softness invites harm.
Greatest Fear:
Being abandoned again and watching Alison inherit the same life.
Greatest Strength:
Astrid endured years of violence and remained fiercely protective of her daughter.
Fatal Flaw:
She directs years of grief and anger at Luke because blaming him is easier than accepting that none of the children had the power to save the others.
Psychological Markers:
Carries anger close to the surface.
Uses painful memories as weapons when cornered.
Distrusts kindness.
Resembles her father in some habits despite hating what he did.
Protects Alison even while struggling to protect herself.
Relationship With Luke:
Astrid loves Luke beneath layers of resentment. She needed him after he left, and part of her never stopped seeing his escape as a personal rejection.
Relationship With Krys:
Astrid initially rejects Krys’s place in Luke’s life, claiming Krys is not part of their family. Krys refuses that division and openly defends Luke.
Locked Thematic Core:
Grief hardened into accusation.
Iris Rawlins
Role: Luke Rawlins’s youngest sister
Family:
Younger sister of Luke and Astrid Rawlins.
Core Spine:
Iris was the most vulnerable of the Rawlins children. She loved Luke, depended upon him for safety, and struggled after he escaped the family home.
Background:
Iris was ten when Luke left at fourteen. As a child, she was frightened of horses and stood behind Luke whenever one passed too close. After he disappeared, she cried for him and called for him at night.
As an adult, Iris developed a serious drinking problem. She died after falling in front of a wagon while drunk and failing to move before the driver could stop. Luke did not learn of her death until the following year, when Tom found him and told him what had happened.
Core Beliefs:
Luke meant safety.
The people she loved could disappear.
Fear was something to endure rather than escape.
Greatest Fear:
Being left alone in danger.
Greatest Strength:
Her childhood love for Luke remained strong despite the years apart.
Fatal Flaw:
Iris never found a healthy way out of the damage created by her childhood and eventually relied on alcohol.
Psychological Importance:
Iris’s death forces Luke to confront the family grief he had buried. He mourns both sisters, admits that he never looked back after leaving, and questions whether he should have returned, despite having been only a child himself.
Relationship With Luke:
Luke remembers Iris as the frightened little sister who trusted him to stand between her and danger. Her death becomes one of his deepest sources of guilt.
Locked Thematic Core:
A frightened child carried into a life she could not outrun.
